How to Write Content that Reading Mode Loves

Short summary: use semantic HTML, a clear headline, and uninterrupted article text so browser reader modes can extract and display your content.

By J. Example • October 8, 2025

Introduction

Reader modes look for a main article block with headings and continuous text. Use <article> / <main> to wrap your story, and keep navigation, sidebars, or unrelated widgets outside the article element.

Structure that Works

Keep paragraphs natural and avoid splitting the main narrative into many tiny fragments. Use semantic headings (<h2>, <h3>) to break sections cleanly. If you must include images, provide descriptive alt text and place images inline near their relevant paragraphs.

Do Not Block Reader Mode

Avoid heavy inline scripts that alter DOM structure before the page fully loads. Many reader implementations disable reader mode when they detect complex app-like pages. Minimal, unobtrusive JS is fine for analytics, but don't inject content asynchronously into the article node.

Example Paragraph

Lorem ipsum dolor sit amet, consectetur adipiscing elit. Integer vitae nunc at sapien dictum volutpat. Vestibulum ante ipsum primis in faucibus orci luctus et ultrices posuere cubilia curae; Sed quis urna id nisl tristique aliquet. Curabitur volutpat, justo eu gravida bibendum, risus neque tincidunt augue, id rhoncus lacus mi a velit.

Subsection: Readability Tips

  • Write meaningful headings and lead paragraphs.
  • Keep a single main content node — the reader prefers that.
  • Use semantic metadata like <time> and itemprop where appropriate.

Conclusion

When readers prefer a clean view, giving the browser clear, semantic signals will increase the chance your content shows up in Reading Mode. Test in Firefox, Safari, and any reader extensions to confirm.
